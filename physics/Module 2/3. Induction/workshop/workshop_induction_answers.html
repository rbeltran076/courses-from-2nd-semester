True or false (and why)

  1. The induced emf is proportional to the magnetic flux going through the circuit.
    • True
  2. While approaching a magnet to a solenoid, the sign of the induced emf depends on which magnetic pole is closer.
    • True
  3. While approaching a magnet to a solenoid, the induced emf depends on the velocity of the magnet.
    • True
  4. Even if the magnetic flux is zero at a some instant, the induced emf can be nonzero.
    • True. What matters is the variation, not the instant value.
  5. Lenz's law is an expression of the conservation of energy.
    • True
  6. The self-inductance depends on the geometry and the current.
    • False. only geometry.
  7. The magnetic energy density in a solenoid depends on its geometry.
    • True
  8. Eddy currents are always a nuisance.
    • False
  9. The superposition principle is valid for non-conservative electric fields.
    • True
  10. Inductors can be used to filter low frequencies.
    • False. high freqs.
  11. Doubling the number of windings in a solenoid results in doubling its self-inductance.
    • False. 4 times
  12. Doubling the number of windings in a solenoid results in doubling its magnetic energy density.
    • False. 4 times

Questions

1.Two solenoids with self-inductances $L_1,L_2$ and mutual inductance $M$ are connected in series. What is the net self-inductance of the system?

Answer: Current is $I_1=I_2=I$ and flux is $\Phi=\Phi_1+\Phi_2=L_1I+L_2I+ 2MI.$ Hence. $L=\Phi/I=L_1+L_2+ 2M$.

2.Two circular loops lie side by side in the same plane. One is connected to a source that supplies an increasing counterclockwise current; the other is a simple closed ring. Is the induced current in the ring clockwise or counterclockwise?

Answer: Counterclockwise.

3.A metal rectangle is close to a long, straight, current-carrying wire, with two of its sides parallel to the wire. If the current in the long wire is decreasing, is the rectangle repelled by or attracted to the wire? Explain why this result is consistent with Lenz's law

Answer: The rectangular wire is attracted to the wire.

4.A metal ring is placed in a uniform magnetic field that increases at a constant rate. If the radius of the ring is doubled, by what factor do the emf induced in the ring change? and the electric field induced in the ring?

Answer: flux increases by four times, so does emf. Electric field only doubles.

5.Is the magnetic energy density stored in a toroidal solenoid greater in vacuum or in a magnetic material?

Answer: with magnetic material, proportional to magnetic permeability

6.Explain what's happening in the following video.

Answer: when the magnets at the edges of the battery touch helical, a closed circuit is created and that portion of the cable becomes a solenoid (which behaves like a magnet). Then, the system effectively behaves as two magnets attracting each other at any given time (similar to question 5 of previous worksheet), resulting in the battery moving along the helical wire.

Problems

Problem 1

How fast would a 50-cm copper bar have to move at right angles to a 1.25-T magnetic field (typical value of modern neodymium magnets) to generate 1.50 V (the same as a AA battery) across its ends? Does this seem like a practical way to generate electricity?

Answer: $v=V/(BL)=8.64km/h$. Not high velocity, but very costly materials for small voltages.

Problem 2

A loop moves along the x-axis at a constant speed $v$. A constant current $I$ flown in a long wire, as shown in the figure. Calculate the emf induced in the loop.

Answer: $B=\mu_0 I/(2\pi r)$. The total flux through the loop is $$ \Phi_{B}=\int d \Phi_{B}=\left(\frac{\mu_{0} I b}{2 \pi}\right) \int_{r}^{r+a} \frac{d x}{x} $$ $$ \begin{array}{l} \Phi_{B}=\left(\frac{\mu_{0} I b}{2 \pi}\right) \ln \left(\frac{r+a}{r}\right) \\ \varepsilon=-\frac{d \Phi_{B}}{d t}=-\frac{d \Phi_{B}}{d r} \frac{d r}{d t}=\frac{\mu_{0} I b}{2 \pi}\left(\frac{a}{r(r+a)}\right) v \end{array} $$ where $$r(t)=r_0+vt$$ We chose the normal vector going into the plane (when calculating the flux), and using the right-hand rule with the normal, as $\varepsilon>0$, the induced current $I=\varepsilon/R$ must be clockwise. Alternatively, $|\varepsilon|=\frac{\mu_{0} I a b v}{2 \pi r(r+a)}$ and using Lenz's law, the current must be clockwise.

Problem 3

A circular loop of flexible iron wire has an initial circumference of $165 cm$, but its circumference is decreasing at a constant rate of $12cm/s$ due to a tangential pull on the wire. The loop is in a constant, uniform magnetic field oriented perpendicular to the plane of the loop and with magnitude $0.5 T$. Find the emf induced in the loop at the instant when $9s$ have passed.

Answer: the circumference is $c=2\pi r$, so the area can be expressed as $A=\pi r^2=c^2/(4\pi)$. Using Faraday's law $$ \begin{array}{l} \Phi_{B}=B A=(B / 4 \pi) c^{2} \\ |\varepsilon|=\left|\frac{d \Phi_{B}}{d t}\right|=\left(\frac{B}{2 \pi}\right) c\left|\frac{d c}{d t}\right| \end{array} $$ $$ \begin{array}{l} \text { At } t=9.0 \mathrm{~s}, c=1.650 \mathrm{~m}-(9.0 \mathrm{~s})(0.120 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})=0.570 \mathrm{~m} \\ |\varepsilon|=(0.500 \mathrm{~T})(1 / 2 \pi)(0.570 \mathrm{~m})(0.120 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})=5.44 \mathrm{mV} \end{array} $$

Using Lenz's law, the current is clockwise. Alternatively, notice that $\varepsilon>0$, so using the right-hand rule with the normal vector going into the plane, it must be clockwise.

Problem 4

A long, thin solenoid has $900$ turns per meter and radius $2.50 cm$. The current in the solenoid is increasing at a uniform rate of $60A/s$. What is the magnitude of the induced electric field at $0.5 cm$ from the axis of the solenoid?

Answer: Let's apply Faraday's law to a circle of radius $r$. $$ \oint \vec{E} \cdot d \vec{l}=-\frac{d \Phi_{B}}{d t} $$ $$ \begin{array}{l} \Phi_{B}=B \pi r^{2} \cdot\left|-\frac{d \Phi_{B}}{d t}\right|=\pi r^{2}\left|\frac{d B}{d t}\right| \\ |\oint \vec{E} \cdot d \vec{l}|=\left|-\frac{d \Phi_{B}}{d t}\right| \implies E(2 \pi r)=\pi r^{2}\left|\frac{d B}{d t}\right| \\ E=\frac{1}{2} r\left|\frac{d B}{d t}\right| \\ B=\mu_{0} n I, \mathrm{so} \frac{d B}{d t}=\mu_{0} n \frac{d I}{d t} \\ \implies E=\frac{1}{2} r \mu_{0} n \frac{d I}{d t}=\frac{1}{2}(0.00500 \mathrm{~m})\left(4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~T} \cdot \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{A}\right)\left(900 \mathrm{~m}^{-1}\right)(60.0 \mathrm{~A} / \mathrm{s})\\ =1.70 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{m} . \end{array} $$

Problem 5

One solenoid is centered inside another. The outer one has a length of 50.0 cm and contains 6750 coils, while the coaxial inner solenoid is 3.0 cm long and 0.120 cm in diameter and contains 15 coils. The current in the outer solenoid is changing at 49.2 A/s. Find

  • a) What is the mutual inductance of these solenoids?
  • b) Find the emf induced in the innner solenoid.

b) $$\varepsilon_{2}=M\left|\frac{d i_{1}}{d t}\right|=\left(2.88 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{H}\right)(49.2 \mathrm{~A} / \mathrm{s})=1.42 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{~V}$$
