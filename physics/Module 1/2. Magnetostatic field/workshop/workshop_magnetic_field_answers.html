Questions

  1. In a Hall-effect experiment, is it possible that no transverse potential difference will be observed? Under what circumstances might this happen?
    • Hall voltage is opposite for opposite charges, so the net Hall effect can be zero.
  2. Hall-effect voltages are much greater for relatively poor conductors (such as germanium) than for good conductors (such as copper), for comparable currents, fields, and dimensions. Why?
    • Charge concentration is smaller in poor conductors, and using the formula one can see that $V_H$ must be higher. A more intuitive explanation: In a poor conductor there are fewer free carriers, so the available carriers have to move faster at a given current. Bearing in mind that the Lorenz force is stronger for higher velocities, it will deflect charges even more towards the side, building up more Hall voltage.
  3. In order to maximize the magnetic moment using a given wire of length $L$, is it better to build a unique circular loop or many of them but smaller ones?
    • Assume we fabricate two loops of length $L'$ from the wire $L$, then $L=2L'$ and $r=2r'$ since $L=2\pi r$. The respective areas are $A=\pi r^2$ and $A'=\pi r'^2$, so $A=4A'$. By definition, $m=IA$ and $m'=IA'$, hence $m=4m'$. Two loops of magnetic moment $m'$ is half the magnetic moment of a single loop, therefore it's better to build just one circular loop.
  4. Infinitely long wires do not exist. Under what circumstances is it justified to state that a particular wire is long enough to be considered infinite?
    • When the distance from the wire is much sorter than the wire length.
  5. Two parallel conductors carrying current in the same direction attract each other. If they are permitted to move toward each other, the forces of attraction do work. From where does the energy come? Does this contradict the assertion that magnetic forces on moving charges do no work?
    • From the energy provided by the batteries causing those currents.
  6. Describe what's happening in the following experiment.
    • The coil magnetize the paramagnetic ball, which is then attracted by the magnetic field produced by the coil. They system behaves as two magnets of opposite poles attracting each other, which reinforces the oscillations.

Problems

Problem 1

We have three infinitely long wires parallel to each other as indicated in the figure. Calculate

  • a) The force per unit length experienced by conductor number $2$.
  • b) The equilibrium position of conductor $2$ if it could freely move on the plane defined by the three wires.
  • c) It such equilibrium stable?

Answer: a) $$\vec B_1=\frac{\mu_0I_1}{2\pi r_1}=-2\times10^{-4}(T)\hat k$$ $$\vec B_3=\frac{\mu_0I_3}{2\pi r_3}=8\times10^{-5}(T)\hat k$$ $$\vec B_{net}=\vec B_1+\vec B_2=-1.2\times 10^{-4}(T)\hat k$$ $$\vec F/l=I_2(-\hat j)\times \vec B_{net}=I_2 B_{net}\hat i=1.2\times 10^{-4}(N/m)\hat i$$

b) $$F_1=F_3\implies\frac{\mu_0I_1I_2}{2\pi r_1}=\frac{\mu_0I_1I_3}{2\pi r_3}\implies\frac{I_3}{r_3}=\frac{I_1}{r_1}=\frac{I_1}{8cm-r_3}\implies r_3=3.2cm$$

c) stable

Problem 2

Two concentric circular loops of wire lie on a tabletop, one inside the other. The inner wire has a diameter of 20.0 cm and carries a clockwise current of 12.0 A, as viewed from above, and the outer wire has a diameter of 30.0 cm. What must be the magnitude and direction (as viewed from above) of the current in the outer wire so that the net magnetic field due to this combination of wires is zero at the common center of the wires?

Answer: $$ B_{1}=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 R_{1}}, B_{2}=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 R_{2}} $$ $$ \frac{\mu_{0} I_{1}}{2 R_{1}}=\frac{\mu_{0} I_{2}}{2 R_{2}} $$ $$ I_{2}=\left(\frac{R_{2}}{R_{1}}\right) I_{1}=\left(\frac{15.0 \mathrm{~cm}}{10.0 \mathrm{~cm}}\right)(12.0 \mathrm{~A})=18.0 \mathrm{~A} $$ $I_2$ is counterclockwise.

Problem 3

A solid conductor with radius $a$ is surrounded by a conducting tube with inner radius $b$ and out radius $c$ as shown in the figure. The central conductor and tube carry equal currents $I$ in opposite directions and uniformly distributed over their cross sections. Find the magnetic field in every point of space.

Answer:

For $r<a$: $$B(2\pi r)=\mu_0(I_1)_{encl}=\mu_0\frac{I}{\pi a^2}(\pi r^2)\implies \vec B=\frac{\mu_{o} I}{2 \pi a^{2}} r\quad\hat u_\varphi$$ For $a<r<b$ $$B(2\pi r)=\mu_0I \implies \vec B=\frac{\mu_{o} I}{2 \pi r} u_\varphi$$ For $b<r<c$ $$B(2\pi r)= \mu_0(I_1-(I_2)_{encl})\implies B=\mu_0(I-\frac{I\pi(r^2-b^2)}{\pi(c^2-b^2)})\implies \vec B=\frac{\mu_{o} I}{2 \pi r}\left(\frac{c^{2}-r^{2}}{c^{2}-b^{2}}\right)\hat u_\varphi $$ For $c<r$ $$B(2\pi r)= \mu_0(I_1-I_2)=0\implies B=0$$

Problem 4

Find the current needed to achieve such a magnetic field of 37.2 T with

  • a) 2.00 cm from a long, straight wire. Answer: $B=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 \pi r}$ so $I=\frac{2 \pi r B}{\mu_{0}}=\frac{2 \pi\left(2.00 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~m}\right)(37.2 \mathrm{~T})}{4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~T} \cdot \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{A}}=3.72 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~A}$
  • b) at the center of a circular coil of radius 42.0 cm that has 100 turns. Answer: $B=\frac{N \mu_{0} I}{2 R}$ so $I=\frac{2 R B}{N \mu_{0}}=\frac{2(0.210 \mathrm{~m})(37.2 \mathrm{~T})}{(100)\left(4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~T} \cdot \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{A}\right)}=1.24 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~A}$
  • c) near the center of a solenoid with radius 2.40 cm, length 32.0 cm, and 40,000 turns. Answer: $B=\mu_{0} \frac{N}{L} I$ so $I=\frac{B L}{\mu_{0} N}=\frac{(37.2 \mathrm{~T})(0.320 \mathrm{~m})}{\left(4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~T} \cdot \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{A}\right)(40,000)}=237 \mathrm{~A}$

Problem 5

A toroidal solenoid with 400 turns of wire and a mean radius of 6.0 cm carries a current of 0.25 A. The relative permeability of the core is 80. What is the magnetic field in the core?

Answer: $B=\frac{\mu N I}{2 \pi r}=\frac{\mu_{\mathrm{m}} \mu_{0} N I}{2 \pi r}=\frac{\mu_{0}(80)(400)(0.25 \mathrm{~A})}{2 \pi(0.060 \mathrm{~m})}=0.0267 \mathrm{~T}$

Problem 6

The figure shows a silver ribbon with $z_1=11.8mm$ and $y_1=0.23mm$ carrying a current of $120 A$ in the positive x-direction. The ribbon lies in a uniform magnetic field in the positive y-direction, with magnitude 0.95 T.

Knowing that for silver there are $5.85\times 10^{28}$ free electrons per cubic meter, find

  • a) the drift velocity of the electrons in the x-direction.
  • b) the magnitude and direction of the electric field in the z-direction due to the Hall effect.
  • c) the Hall emf.

Answer

a) Using the definition of the current density

$$ J_{x}=\frac{I}{A}=\frac{I}{y_{1} z_{1}}=\frac{120 \mathrm{~A}}{\left(0.23 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~m}\right)(0.0118 \mathrm{~m})}=4.42 \times 10^{7} \mathrm{~A} / \mathrm{m}^{2} $$

Additionally, we can relate the current density with the drift velocity of electrons.

$$ v_{\mathrm{d}}=\frac{J_{x}}{nq}=\frac{4.42 \times 10^{7} \mathrm{A} / \mathrm{m}^{2}}{\left(5.85 \times 10^{28} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left(-1.602 \times 10^{-19} \mathrm{C}\right)}=-4.7 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{s}=-4.7 \mathrm{~mm} / \mathrm{s} $$

b) As for the module of the electric field, we know that the magnetic and electric forces must be equal in module with opposite directions (in equilibrium). Then,

$$ \begin{array}{l} |q E_{z}|=|q v_{\mathrm{d}} B_{y}| \\ |E_{z}|=|v_{\mathrm{d}} B_{y}|=\left(4.7 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\right)(0.95 \mathrm{~T})=4.5 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{m} \end{array} $$

Using the right-hand rule, the magnetic force is along the positive z-axis, which means that the electric force must be along the negative z-axis. As the charge carrier is negative (electrons), the electric field must be in the positive z-axis. All in all,

$$\vec E=+4.5 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{m}\quad\hat z$$

c) The Hall emf is the potential difference between the two edges of the strip (from $z=0$ and $z=z_1$) that results from the electric field calculated in part b). $V_{hall} =Ez_1 =(4.5 \times 10^{-3} V/m)(0 0118 m)=53 \mu V$

Problem 7

Imagine a strip of an unknown metal of the same dimensions as those of problem 6. When the magnetic field is $2.29 T$ and the current is $78.0 A$, the Hall emf is found to be $\mathcal{E}= 131\mu V$. What is the density of free electrons in the unknown metal according to the Hall effect?

Answer

$$ n=\frac{J_{x} B_{y}}{|q| E_{z}}=\frac{I B_{y}}{A|q| E_{z}}=\frac{I B_{y} z_{1}}{A|q| \mathcal{E}}=\frac{I B_{y}}{y_{1}|q| \mathcal{E}} $$$$ n=\frac{(78.0 \mathrm{~A})(2.29 \mathrm{~T})}{\left(2.3 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}\right)\left(1.6 \times 10^{-19} \mathrm{C}\right)\left(1.31 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~V}\right)}=3.7 \times 10^{28} \mathrm{m}^{-3} $$
