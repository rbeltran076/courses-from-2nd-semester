Workshop module 1: Electric field

True or false (and why)

  1. Insulators can be charged by induction.
    • False. Only conductors
  2. An electrically charged insulator and a neutral conductor attract each other.
    • True (by polarization of charges in the conductor)
  3. The electric force exerted on $q_1$ by $q_2$ does not change even if another charge $q_3$ shows up.
    • True. Superposition principle.
  4. The acceleration of a charge located at a certain point in space where there exists an electric field lies in the direction of the electric field line that passes through that point.
    • True. Second Newton's law.
  5. A body cannot create an electric field unless it has a net electric charge.
    • False. Think of a dipole.
  6. A dipole whose moment is perpendicular to an external electric field does not move.
    • False. It rotates.
  7. The existence of a nucleus contradicts the Coulomb force.
    • True. That's why we need nuclear forces.
  8. If the electric flux through a closed surface is zero then there cannot be any charge inside the surface.
    • False. There can be charges as long as the net charge is zero.
  9. If the electric flux through a closed surface is zero, the electric field must be zero at any point over that surface.
    • False.
  10. If the electric flux through a closed surface is zero, then number of field lines going out of the surface must equal the number of lines going in.
    • True
  11. Gauss law is only valid for symmetrical charge distributions.
    • False.
  12. A gaussian surface is one such that the electric field is constant over it.
    • False
  13. Close enough to a uniformly charged disk, the electric field resembles the field created by an infinitely charged sheet.
    • True
  14. If the electric field is zero at any point of a closed surface, then there must be no net charge inside the surface.
    • True
  15. The electric potential can be positive or negative depending on the sign of the source charge.
    • True, by definition
  16. The net potential at any given point created by a collection of charges is the vectorial sum of the potential created by each charge independently.
    • False. Algebraic sum.
  17. It's impossible to have a negative electric potential between the space of two positive charges.
    • False. Potential depends on the chosen origin of energies.
  18. The potential energy of a collection of charges is $U=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \sum_{i,j} \frac{q_{i} q_{j}}{r_{i j}}$.
    • False. it should be $\sum_{i<j}$ to avoid double counting pairs.
  19. We put three identical charges in the vertices of a triangle. In the center of the triangle, both the electric field and the potential are zero.
    • False. Electric field is zero, but not the potential.
  20. In presence of an electric field, it's impossible to move a charge without doing work against the field.
    • False. You could move the charge perpendicular to the field.
  21. Capacitor 1 has twice as much capacitance as capacitor 2 given the same charge. Therefore, capacitor 1 has also twice as much voltage as capacitor 2
    • False. Voltage in 2 must be half.
  22. Capacitor 1 has twice as much charge as capacitor 2 given the same voltage. Consequently, the energy stored in capacitor 2 doubles the energy of capacitor 1.
    • False. Energy in 1 doubles 2
  23. Capacitance decreases when a dielectric is put between the plates of a capacitor.
    • False. It increases
  24. Given a capacitor connected to a battery, its charge decreases when a dielectric is put between its plates.
    • False. Dielectric gets polarized the capacitor admits more charge to keep the electric field between the plates.

Questions

  1. Suppose you have a positive charge $q$ that you want to distribute in two different bodies. How do you have to distribute the electrical charge between them so that electrical repulsion is maximum?
    • Electric force is proportional to the product of charges $F\propto (q-x)x\implies dF/dx\propto(q-2x)$. Then $dF/dx=0\implies x=q/2$. Equal distribution between bodies.
  2. Choose the figure that best represents Coulomb force as a function of distance.
    • A. $F\propto 1/r^2$.
  3. The dielectric strength of air is $3 MV/m$, what should be the minimum separation between the plates of a capacitor to avoid dielectric breakdown given a voltage of $4kV$?
    • minimum separation translates into maximum electric field between the plates, which can be written in terms of voltage as $d_{min}=\Delta V/E_{max}=3\times 10^3(V)/4\times 10^6(V/m)=0.75mm$.
  4. A charge $Q_1$ is located on the positive $x$ axis, and another charge $Q_2$ is located on the same axis but further away from the origin $O$. Knowing that the total electric charge is zero in $O$, what can we state about the signs of the charges? and what about their magnitudes?
    • For the net electric field to be zero at the origin, signs of charges must be opposite. Additionally, the smallest charge should be closer to the origin so $|Q_2|>|Q_1|$.
  5. A balloon of radius $R$ if full of a gas with charge $Q$. The balloon is injected with another neutral gas so that the radius doubles. How does the electric field $E$ vary at a distance $R$ from the center of the balloon?
    • $\rho_1=\frac{Q}{4/3\pi R^3}$ and $\rho_2=\frac{Q}{4/3\pi (2R)^3}$. Then $\rho_1=8\rho_2$. Gauss law implies $E_1/E_2=\rho_1/\rho_2=8$
  6. For parallel-plate capacitor with $d=1mm$, how much surface do we need to store $1F$?
    • $A=Cd/\epsilon_0=1F\times 10^{-3}m/8.85\times10^{-12}(F/m)=10km^2$. A square of side $10km$.
  7. A little metallic sphere is placed between the plates of a capacitor. What is the value of the electric field and the potential inside the sphere?
    • Free charges inside the sphere rearange so that the electric field inside the sphere is zero, and the potential is constant.
  8. Discuss what happens to a charged parallel-plate capacitor when the distance $d$ is reduced to a half, in terms of its capacitance, voltage, electric field, and stored energy. Repeat the same analysis when the capacitor is connected to a battery.
    • Capacitor is isolated so $Q$ is constant. $C'=2C$ and $V'=V/2$. $E'=V'/d'=E$ and $U'=1/2QV'=U/2$ but $u'=1/2\epsilon_0E'^2=u$.

Problem 1

At the end points of a straight tube of length $L$ there are two charges $Q_1$ and $Q_2=2Q_1$, respectively. A small metallic sphere of charge $Q$ lies within, which can freely move along the tube.

Find:

  • a) The equilibrium position $x$ of the sphere.
  • b) is the equilibrium stable?
  • c) it would be stable without the tube?
  • d) it would be stable if the charge $Q$ were negative?

Answer:

  • a) $\frac{kQ_1Q}{x^2}=\frac{kQ_2Q}{(l-x)^2}\implies \frac{1}{x^2}=\frac{2}{(l-x)^2}\implies x\simeq 0.41l$
  • b) the equilibrium is stable
  • c) it would not be stable
  • d) it would not be stable

Problem 2

Positive charge Q is uniformly distributed along the positive $y$-axis between as indicated in the figure. Another point charge $q$ is located on the $x$-axis at $x=a$. Calculate the electric field experienced by $q$.

Answer One way: calculate the electric potential and then take the gradient. Here we rely on a direct integration of the electric field. Two approaches to get the same answer seen in the workshop, a "geometric approach" and a "systematic approach" (I personally prefer the latter one).

The geometric approach: The module of the electric field at $x$ created by an infinitesimal charge $dq$ located at $y$ is

$$ d E=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}}\frac{dq}{x^2+y^2}$$

What is $dq$? As we have a linear charge distribution, then by definition $dq=\lambda dL$. And what is dL in this case? dL=dy. Additionally, it's straightforward to notice that $\lambda=Q/L$ in this case. Thus,

$$ dE=\frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}L}\frac{dy}{x^2+y^2}$$

Now, in looking at the figure, the components of the vector (the electric field is a vector, not its module!) must be

$$ \begin{array}{l} d E_{x}=d E \cos \theta=\frac{Q x}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} L}\left(\frac{d y}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}}\right) \\ d E_{y}=-d E \sin \theta=-\frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} L}\left(\frac{y d y}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}}\right) \\ \end{array} $$

where

$$ \begin{array}{l} \sin \theta=\frac{y}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \\ \cos \theta=\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \end{array} $$

Integrating we obtain the electric field ($\vec E=E_x\hat i+E_y \hat j$)

$$ \begin{array}{l} E_{x}=\int d E_{x}=\frac{Q x}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} L} \int_{0}^{L} \frac{d y}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}}=\frac{Q x}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} L}\left[\frac{1}{x^{2}} \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\right]_{0}^{L}=\frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} x} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+L^{2}}} \\ E_{y}=\int d E y=-\frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} L} \int_{0}^{L} \frac{y d y}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}}=-\frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} L}\left[-\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\right]_{0}^{L}=-\frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} L}\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+L^{2}}}\right) \end{array} $$

The systematic approach:

The general expression for the electric field created by $dq$ is

$$ d\vec E=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}}\frac{dq(\vec r-\vec r')}{|\vec r-\vec r'|^3}$$

Let's identify terms

$$\begin{array} \vec r=x\hat i \\ \vec r'=y\hat j \\ \vec r-\vec r'=x\hat i-y\hat j \\ |\vec r-\vec r'|^3=(x^2+y^2)^{3/2} \\ dq=\lambda dL=Qdy/L \\ \end{array}$$

Hence

$$ d\vec E=\frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}L}\frac{dy(x\hat i-y\hat j)}{(x^2+y^2)^{3/2}}$$

Integrating the previous expression we obtain the same results as before.

Problem 3

Calculate the electric field at any point created by an infinitely large cylinder of radius $R$ with a charge density

$$\begin{array}{ll}\rho(r)=\rho_{0}(1-r / R) & \text { for } r \leq R \\ \rho(r)=0 & \text { for } r \geq R\end{array}$$

where $r$ is the distance from the axis of the cylinder.

Answer:

By choosing a cylinder of radius $r$ as a gaussian surface (over which the electric field is constant), applying the Gauss law we obtain

$$\int \vec E\cdot dS=E(2\pi rL)=Q_{enc}/\epsilon_0$$

$r>R$: $$Q_{enc}=Q_{Total}=\int_0^R\rho(r)dV=\rho_0\int_0^R(1-r/R)(2\pi rLdr)=2\pi\rho_0L(\frac{R^2}{2}-\frac{R^3}{3R})=\pi/3\rho_0LR^2$$ $$E(2\pi rL)=\pi\rho_0 LR^2/(3\epsilon_0)\implies \vec E=\frac{\rho_0R^2}{6\epsilon_0r}\hat r$$ $r<R$: $$Q_{enc}=\int_0^r\rho(r')dV=\rho_0\int_0^r(1-r'/R)(2\pi r'Ldr')=2\pi\rho_0L(\frac{r^2}{2}-\frac{r^3}{3R})$$ $$E(2\pi rL)=\frac{2\pi\rho_0L}{\epsilon_0}(\frac{r^2}{2}-\frac{r^3}{3R})\implies \vec E=\frac{\rho_0}{\epsilon_0}(\frac{r}{2}-\frac{r^2}{3R})\hat r$$

Problem 4

Three point charges are located on the $x$-axis. $Q_1=2nC$ at $x=0$, $Q_2=-3nC$ at $x=20cm$ and $Q_3=5nC$ at $x=10cm$. Assume that the energy is zero when charges are infinitely separated.

  • a) What is the potential energy of this configuration?
  • b) Having fixed $Q_1$ and $Q_2$, where should I place $Q_3$ so that the total energy is zero?

Answer:

  • a) $U=_{ij}=k\frac{Q_iQ_i}{r_{ij}}$ so $U_{12}=-2.7\times 10^{-7}J$,$U_{13}=9.0\times 10^{-7}J$,$U_{23}=-13.5\times 10^{-7}J$. $U=-7.2\times 10^{-7}J$
  • b) let $x=r_{13}\implies r_{23}=0.2(m)-x$. Then $U(x)=k\frac{Q_1Q_2}{0.2}+k\frac{Q_2Q_3}{(0.2-x)}+k\frac{Q_1Q_3}{x}\implies 30x^2-31x+2=0\implies x_1=6.9cm,x_2=96.4cm$

Problem 5

The electric potential in a certain region of space is given by $V(x,y)=ax^2+bxy+cy$, with $a=3V/m^2,b=-2V/m^2,c=5V/m^2$. Find

  • a) The electric field $\vec E(x,y)$.
  • b) The work done by the electric field when moving a proton from $x=0m$ to $x=1m$.

Answer:

  • a) $\vec E=-\nabla V=-(2ax+by)\hat i-(bx+c)\hat j$
  • b) Using the electric potential $W=-\Delta U=-e(V(x=1m,y=0m)-V(x=0m,y=0m))=-3(eV)$. Using the definition of work, $W=\int_{x=0m}^{x=1m}e\vec E(x,y=0)\cdot d\vec l=-2ae[x^2/2]_{x=0m}^{x=1m}=-3(eV)$

Problem 6

A parallel-plate capacitor with area $A=500cm^2$ and separation $d=5mm$ has a voltage of $V_0=2000V$. Introducing a dielectric between its plates reduces the voltage to $V=1000V$. Find

  • a) The capacity after introducing the dielectric.
  • b) The relative permittivity.
  • c) The induced charge on each surface of the dielectric.

Answer:

  • a) Before we introduce the dielectric, we can write $C_0=Q_0/V_0$ and $C_0=\epsilon_0A/d$. From these two equations we can obtain the charge $Q_0=(\epsilon_0A/d)V_0=1.7\times 10^{-7}C$.

Once the dielectric is introduced, the charge on the metal plates remains the same ($Q_0$), although the difference of potential does change (bear in mind that the capacitor is isolated,not connected to any battery). Therefore $C=Q_0/V=1.7\times 10^{-7}C/1000V=1.7\times 10^{-4}\mu F$

  • b) Applying the definition of relative permittivity, $\epsilon_r=C/C_0=V_0/V=2$.
  • c) The dielectric gets polarized after being introduced between the plates of the capacitor. This means that a new superficial charge distribution appears on the surface of the dielectric, $\sigma_i$, with opposite sign with respect to the charge of the metal plate. The module of the electric field between the plates will be now
$$E=\frac{\sigma-\sigma_i}{\epsilon_0},$$

where $\sigma=Q_0/A$ and $\sigma_i=Q_i/A$.

The relative permittivity can be calculated from

$$\epsilon_r=\frac{V_0}{V}=\frac{E_0}{E}=\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-\sigma_i}\implies\sigma_i=\sigma\left(1-\frac{1}{\epsilon_r}\right)\implies Q_i=Q_0\left(1-\frac{1}{\epsilon_r}\right)=8.5\times 10^{-7}C$$

Problem 7

Suppose we connect a couple of capacitors in series, with capacitance of $150nF$ and $120nF$ respectively. Knowing that the potential difference across them is $36V$. Find:

  • a) the total charge stored in the network.
  • b) the charge on each capacitor.
  • c) the total energy stored in the network.
  • d) the energy stored in each capacitor.
  • e) the potential differences across each capacitor.

Answer

The capacitors are in series, so the overall capacitance of the network is

$$ C_{\mathrm{eq}}=\frac{C_{1} C_{2}}{C_{1}+C_{2}}=\frac{(150 \mathrm{nF})(120 \mathrm{nF})}{150 \mathrm{nF}+120 \mathrm{nF}}=66.7 \mathrm{nF} $$

a) The total charge is then $$ Q=C V=(66.7 \mathrm{nF})(36 \mathrm{~V})=2.4 \times 10^{-6} \mathrm{C}=2.4 \mu \mathrm{C} $$

b) As they are in series, each capacitor will store the same amount of electrical charge on its plates regardless of its capacitance. This is because the charge stored by a plate of any one capacitor must have come from the plate of its adjacent capacitor. $Q_1=Q_2=2.4 \mu \mathrm{C}$

c) $$ U=\frac{1}{2} C_{\mathrm{eq}} V^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(66.7 \times 10^{-9} \mathrm{~F}\right)(36 \mathrm{~V})^{2}=43.2 \mu \mathrm{J} $$

d) We know $C$ and $Q$ for each capacitor, and we can write $U$ in term of these

$$ U=\frac{1}{2} C V^{2}=\frac{1}{2} C(Q / C)^{2}=Q^{2} / 2 C $$$$ 150 \mathrm{nF}: U=\frac{\left(2.4 \times 10^{-6} \mathrm{C}\right)^{2}}{2\left(150 \times 10^{-9} \mathrm{~F}\right)}=19.2 \mu \mathrm{J} ; 120 \mathrm{nF}: U=\frac{\left(2.4 \times 10^{-6} \mathrm{C}\right)^{2}}{2\left(120 \times 10^{-9} \mathrm{~F}\right)}=24.0 \mu \mathrm{J} $$

The sum of these two energies is obviously the total energy from c)

e) $$ 150 \mathrm{nF}: V=\frac{Q}{C}=\frac{2.4 \times 10^{-6} \mathrm{C}}{150 \times 10^{-9} \mathrm{~F}}=16 \mathrm{~V} ; 120 \mathrm{nF}: V=\frac{Q}{C}=\frac{2.4 \times 10^{-6} \mathrm{C}}{120 \times 10^{-9} \mathrm{~F}}=20 \mathrm{~V} $$

Notice that these values add up to $36V$, the voltage across the capacitors in series.

Problem 8

Electronic flash units for cameras contain a capacitor for storing the energy used to produce the flash. In one such unit, the flash lasts for $(1/675)$ seconds with an average light power output of $2.7\times 10^5 W$.

  • a) If the conversion of electrical energy to light is 95% efficient (the rest of the energy goes to thermal energy), how much energy must be stored in the capacitor for one flash?
  • b) If the capacitor has a potential difference between its plates of 125V, what is the capacitance?

Answer

a) The power output is $2.70 \times 10^5 W$ and 95% of the original energy is converted. Thus,

$$ E=P t=\left(2.70 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~W}\right)\left(1.48 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~s}\right)=400 \mathrm{~J} . \quad \implies U=\frac{400 \mathrm{~J}}{0.95}=421 \mathrm{~J} $$

b) $$ U=\frac{1}{2} C V^{2} \implies C=\frac{2 U}{V^{2}}=\frac{2(421 \mathrm{~J})}{(125 \mathrm{~V})^{2}}=0.054 \mathrm{~F} $$

Problem 9

A parallel-plate capacitor is made from two plates $12 cm$ on each side and $4.50 mm$ apart. Half of the space between these plates contains only air, but the other half is filled with Plexiglas of relative permittivity 3.40 as shown below. An 18.0-V battery is connected across the plates.

  • a) What is the capacitance of this combination? Assume this capacitor as equivalent to two capacitors in parallel
  • b) How much energy is stored in the capacitor?
  • c) If we remove the Plexiglas but change nothing else, how much energy will be stored in the capacitor?

Answer

The capacitor is equivalent to two capacitors in parallel, and each of these two capacitors have plates that are $12.0 cm$ by $6.0 cm$.

a) $$C_{2}=\epsilon_{0} \frac{A}{d}=\frac{\left(8.854 \times 10^{-12} \mathrm{F} \mathrm{m}\right)(0.120 \mathrm{m})(0.060 \mathrm{m})}{4.50 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{m}}=1.42 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{F} . \\ C_{1}=\epsilon_r C_{2}=(3.40)\left(1.42 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{F}\right)=4.83 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{F} . \\ C=C_{1}+C_{2}=6.25 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{F}=62.5 \mathrm{pF} $$

b) $$ U=\frac{1}{2} C V^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(6.25 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{F}\right)(18.0 \mathrm{V})^{2}=1.01 \times 10^{-8} \mathrm{J} $$

c) After removing the dielectric, now we have that $C_1=C_2$. so

$$ C=2\left(1.42 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{F}\right)=2.84 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{F} $$$$ U=\frac{1}{2} C V^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(2.84 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{~F}\right)(18.0 \mathrm{~V})^{2}=4.60 \times 10^{-9} \mathrm{~J} $$
